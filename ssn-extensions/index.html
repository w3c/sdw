The Semantic Sensor Network (SSN) ontology is an ontology for describing sensors and their observations, the involved procedures, the studied features of interest, the samples used to do so, and the observed properties, as well as actuators. This document describes some extensions to the SSN ontology to enable:

  1. linking to the ultimate feature-of-interest for an observation, alongside the link to the proximate feature-of-interest which may be a sample
  2. homogeneous collections of observations, in which one or more of the feature-of-interest, observed-property, procedure, sensor, phenomenon-time or result-time may be common for the whole collection

The namespace for SSN terms is http://www.w3.org/ns/ssn/.
The namespace for SOSA terms is http://www.w3.org/ns/sosa/.

The suggested prefix for the SSN namespace is ssn.
The suggested prefix for the SOSA namespace is sosa.

The SSN ontology is available at http://www.w3.org/ns/ssn/.

New classes and properties are introduced in this extension to SSN. The new elements primarily relate to additional requirements for describing observations and collections of observations. The ontology document <owl:imports> the SSN ontology, and adds the new elements and axioms in the original RDF namespaces.

Motivation and background

The Semantic Sensor Network (SSN) ontology is an ontology for describing sensors and their observations, the involved procedures, the studied features of interest, the samples used to do so, and the observed properties, as well as actuators. The elements of the ontology are defined in two modules:

  1. SOSA - which defines the basic classes and properties, with a weak axiomatization comparable to schema.org
  2. SSN - which adds a full axiomatization to the SOSA classes and properties, and adds additional elements relating particularly to sensor descriptions and to support alignment to DUL

However, a nummber of requirements for describing observations that have been described in the research literature [[OM-Lite]], [[OBOE]] are not supported directly by SSN. In particular:

  1. discovery and use of observation data typically depends on them being related to an "ultimate" feature-of-interest which is a well-knonw thing in the world, such as a geological formation, or a specific patient, or the state of the atmosphere. However, this may not be directly associated with the individual observations, if the (proximate) feature-of-interest of the observation is a sample of the ultimate feature-of-interest. While the identity of the ultimate feature-of-interest may be obtained by following additional sosa:isSampleOf relationships, chasing down such a path may not be possible in a single request from many data sources. A direct association from the observation to the ultimate feature-of-interest alongside the proximate feature-of-interest would be helpful and would properly reflect the intention of the observation;
  2. observations are often made as part of a set or collection in which one or more of the usual observation properties - feature-of-interest, observed-property, sensor, procedure, phenomenon-time, result-time - are common for all members of the collection. It is helpful to be able to package such a homogeneous collection in a standardized way, to support efficient discovery and data transfer.
